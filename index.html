What is this?

What Civic Conscience does

Civic Conscience helps governments, institutions, mediators, and civic leaders understand how different stakeholders may respond to sensitive decisions.

Instead of treating conflict as a communications problem only, CIQ examines the deeper moral signals beneath public reactions: trust, fairness, identity, authority, belonging, grievance, sacred values, and reconciliation readiness.

Scenario-dependent ECC drift

The key insight: people shift under pressure

A stakeholder's civic conscience profile is not static. A group may appear highly pluralistic in normal discussion, but become less pluralistic when identity, dignity, sacred values, or safety feel threatened.

Scenario-dependent ECC drift shows how stakeholder responses change under different moral conditions.

Condition Pluralism Authority Identity Threat Fragmentation Risk
Normal public discussion High Medium Low Low
After sacred-value insult Low High High Severe
After inclusive consultation Medium-high Medium Medium Moderate
After trusted mediator intervention High Medium Low Low

This helps leaders see not only where conflict may emerge, but what conditions may reduce it.

Validation ladder

Synthetic modelling is a starting layer, not proof

CIQ can begin with synthetic modelling for early civic rehearsal, but the system becomes stronger when profiles are calibrated against expert judgment, stakeholder data, consultation evidence, and real-world outcomes.

  1. Synthetic ECC profileUseful for early scenario exploration.
  2. Expert-calibrated ECC profileStronger.
  3. Stakeholder-validated ECC profileMuch stronger.
  4. Outcome-calibrated ECC profileDefensible moat.

Defensibility

The moat is the measurement system, not the model

A generic LLM can imitate stakeholder language. It cannot easily reproduce CIQ's calibrated ECC ontology, aggregation rules, red-line database, scenario library, calibration process, institutional reports, expert review workflow, and historical predicted-vs-actual outcome dataset.
